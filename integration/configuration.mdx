title: "Set up your config"

Everything about how Sentinel works with your robot lives in one config file: which topics to use, your robot's kinematics, how motion is smoothed, safety limits, button mappings, and which cameras to stream.

You don't write this file from scratch. Pick the template that matches your robot's shape, fill in the `<EDIT>` fields, and launch. The templates are complete files — every value you don't touch is a working default.

## Pick a template

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Single arm + gripper">
    One arm behind a `ros2_control` forward position controller (or a
    `JointTrajectory` topic — the template shows both), a gripper on its own
    topic, one camera.

    <a href="/configs/ros2_single_arm.yaml" download>⬇ Download `ros2_single_arm.yaml`</a>

    The part that maps Sentinel onto your topics:

    ```yaml
    bridge:
      manipulator:
        command:
          outputs:                                          # forward position controller
            - topic: "/forward_position_controller/commands"
              qos: "reliable"
              joint_names: [joint_1, joint_2, joint_3,      # your controller's
                            joint_4, joint_5, joint_6]      # configured joint order
        state:
          topic: "/joint_states"                            # must always be flowing
          qos: "sensor"
          stale_timeout_s: 0.5                              # dead stream -> e-stop

      gripper:
        command:
          message_type: "float64"
          topic: "/gripper/command"
          mapping: {mode: "affine", offset: 0.0, scale: 0.8}  # 0-100% -> your range
    ```
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Dual arm (bimanual)">
    Two arms behind one ROS 2 stack: one forward position controller per arm,
    one shared `/joint_states`, grippers carried as the trailing slot of each
    arm's command array (swap in per-gripper topics if yours are separate),
    three cameras.

    <a href="/configs/ros2_dual_arm.yaml" download>⬇ Download `ros2_dual_arm.yaml`</a>

    The part that maps Sentinel onto your topics:

    ```yaml
    bridge:
      manipulator:
        command:
          outputs:                                          # one array stream per arm
            - topic: "/left_forward_position_controller/commands"
              qos: "reliable"
              joint_names: [left_joint_1, ..., left_gripper_joint]
            - topic: "/right_forward_position_controller/commands"
              qos: "reliable"
              joint_names: [right_joint_1, ..., right_gripper_joint]
        state:
          topic: "/joint_states"                            # both arms, one stream
          qos: "sensor"
          stale_timeout_s: 0.5

      gripper:
        command:
          message_type: "manipulator_slot"                  # gripper rides in the array
          left_joint: "left_gripper_joint"
          right_joint: "right_gripper_joint"
          mapping: {mode: "affine", offset: 0.0, scale: 0.05}
    ```
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

<Note>
  Different shape — mobile base, neck, dexterous hand, elevator, PTZ? Start
  from the closest template; each extra capability is one more block in the
  `bridge:` section. [Ask us on Slack](https://avea-robotics.slack.com) and
  we'll send you the block for it.
</Note>

## Fill in the marked fields

Every field you need to change is marked `<EDIT>` in the file. There are five:

<Steps>
  <Step title="License key">
    Generate one in the [dashboard](https://dashboard.avearobotics.com/dashboard/robots) and paste it into `system.license_key`.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Your URDF">
    Point the `description:` block at your robot model — a latched `robot_description` topic, a `robot_state_publisher` parameter, or a file you mount. See [the control interface](/integration/robot-adapter#first-your-robot-description-urdf).
  </Step>
  <Step title="Topics and joint names">
    Your command topic(s), your `/joint_states` topic, your camera topic(s), and your joint names exactly as they appear in your URDF. In forward-position mode the `joint_names` list under each output is the array's slot order, so it has to match your controller's configured joint order.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Joint limits and poses">
    `q_min`/`q_max` from your URDF, plus `home_positions`/`start_positions` — where "go home" sends the arm.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Gripper range">
    The `mapping:` under the gripper block converts Sentinel's normalized 0 (closed) to 100 (open) into your gripper's real travel, so you don't hard-code limits in your controller.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Warning>
  Leave the `orchestration:` section alone. It sequences the runtime's nodes
  through arm, disarm, and teleop — including the disarm teardown that stops
  the command stream and lets a state-stream fault recover on re-arm.
</Warning>

## Launch and tune

Drop the file in as your `robot.yaml` and [install and run](/installation) the runtime. Confirm your joint state is flowing, **arm** the robot, run a homing move, then **start teleoperation**.

The motion sections (`trajectory:` limits, retargeting `scale`, IK weights) ship conservative, so the robot feels slow at first. The runtime smooths every command through those limits before it reaches your controller, and we raise them with you during bring-up until tracking feels right.

<Tip>
  The config isn't frozen. When we tune motion, adjust limits, remap a button,
  or add a camera, only the config changes — your robot's controllers stay the
  same, as long as the topics stay the same.
</Tip>
